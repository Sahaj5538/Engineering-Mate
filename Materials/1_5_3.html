1. Batteries

Batteries are devices that convert chemical energy into electrical energy. They consist of one or more electrochemical cells, each containing a positive electrode (cathode), a negative electrode (anode), and an electrolyte.

Primary and Secondary Batteries:

  • Primary Batteries: Non-rechargeable, used once. Examples: zinc-carbon, alkaline batteries.
  • Secondary Batteries: Rechargeable, used multiple times. Examples: lithium-ion, lead-acid batteries.

Lithium-Ion Battery:

  • Working Principle: Lithium ions move from anode to cathode during discharge and reverse during charging.
  • Components: Lithium metal oxide cathode (e.g., LiCoO2), graphite anode, lithium salt electrolyte.
  • Advantages: High energy density, low self-discharge, no memory effect.
  • Disadvantages: Expensive, safety concerns (thermal runaway).

Lead-Acid Battery:

  • Working Principle: Chemical reaction between lead dioxide (PbO2) and spongy lead (Pb) with sulfuric acid (H2SO4).
  • Components: Lead dioxide cathode, spongy lead anode, sulfuric acid electrolyte.
  • Advantages: Low cost, high surge current capability, reliable performance.
  • Disadvantages: Low energy density, heavy weight, environmental concerns (lead content).

2. Fuel Cells

Fuel cells are electrochemical devices that convert the chemical energy of a fuel (e.g., hydrogen) and an oxidant (e.g., oxygen) into electricity.

Definition:

A fuel cell continuously converts the chemical energy of a fuel and an oxidant into electrical energy.

Hydrogen-Oxygen Fuel Cell:

  • Working Principle: Hydrogen oxidized at anode; protons migrate through the electrolyte; react with oxygen at cathode forming water.
  • Reactions:
    • Anode: 2H2 → 4H+ + 4e-
    • Cathode: O2 + 4H+ + 4e- → 2H2O
    • Overall: 2H2 + O2 → 2H2O

Applications:

  • Transportation
  • Stationary power generation
  • Portable power devices

Microbial Fuel Cell:

  • Working Principle: Microorganisms oxidize organic matter, generating electrons and protons. Electrons transfer to anode; protons move to cathode to react with oxygen, forming water.
  • Applications: Wastewater treatment, bioenergy production, environmental monitoring.

3. Corrosion

Corrosion is the degradation of a material due to chemical or electrochemical reactions with its environment.

Definition, Causes, and Effects of Corrosion:

  • Definition: Deterioration of material, usually metal, due to environmental reactions.
  • Causes: Moisture, acids, salts, pollutants.
  • Effects: Loss of structural integrity, reduced performance, increased maintenance costs.

Theories of Corrosion:

  • Chemical Corrosion: Direct chemical attack without electric current.
  • Electrochemical Corrosion: Involves electric current flow between anodic and cathodic areas.

Mechanism:

  • Anodic Reaction: Metal atoms lose electrons and dissolve into electrolyte.
  • Cathodic Reaction: Electrons are consumed by a reduction reaction (e.g., oxygen reduction or hydrogen evolution).

Types of Corrosion:

  • Differential Metal Corrosion (Galvanic Corrosion): Two dissimilar metals contact in presence of an electrolyte; more active metal corrodes.
  • Differential Aeration Corrosion (Pitting Corrosion): Oxygen concentration differences on metal surface lead to localized corrosion.

Factors Affecting Corrosion:

Nature of Metal:

  • Reactivity Series Position: More reactive metals corrode easily.
  • Relative Areas: Small anodic area and large cathodic area accelerate corrosion at anode.
  • Purity of Metal: Impurities can form local galvanic cells, speeding up corrosion.
  • Passivity: Some metals form a protective oxide layer (e.g., aluminum, stainless steel).

Nature of Environment:

  • pH: Acidic conditions increase corrosion rates.
  • Temperature: Higher temperature accelerates corrosion.
  • Humidity: Moisture is essential for electrochemical corrosion.

4. Corrosion Control Methods

Corrosion control methods are techniques used to prevent or minimize corrosion.

Cathodic Protection:

  • Sacrificial Anodic Protection: A more active metal corrodes preferentially, protecting the base metal.
  • Impressed Current Cathodic Protection: An external power source makes the structure cathodic, preventing corrosion.

Metallic Coatings:

Hot Dipping:

  • Galvanization: Steel is coated with zinc. Zinc corrodes preferentially, protecting steel underneath.
  • Tinning: Steel is coated with tin, providing a corrosion-resistant barrier.
