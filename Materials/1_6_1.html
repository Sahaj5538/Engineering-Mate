Electrical Quantities

Understanding the basic electrical quantities is essential for analyzing circuits.

Charge (Q)

  • Definition: A fundamental property of matter causing a force in an electromagnetic field.
  • Unit: Coulomb (C)

Current (I)

  • Definition: The rate of flow of electric charge through a conductor.
  • Unit: Ampere (A), where 1 A = 1 C/s

Voltage (V)

  • Definition: The electric potential difference between two points in a circuit, representing the energy needed to move a unit charge.
  • Unit: Volt (V), where 1 V = 1 J/C

Power (P)

  • Definition: The rate at which electrical energy is transferred or consumed.
  • Unit: Watt (W), where 1 W = 1 J/s
  • Equation: P = VI

Components

Components in an electric circuit can be classified as passive or active.

Passive Components

These components do not generate energy but can store or dissipate it.

  • Resistor (R): Opposes current flow, dissipates energy as heat. Unit: Ohm (Ω)
  • Inductor (L): Stores energy in a magnetic field. Unit: Henry (H)
  • Capacitor (C): Stores energy in an electric field. Unit: Farad (F)

Sources

Independent Sources

  • Voltage Source: Provides constant voltage regardless of current.
  • Current Source: Provides constant current regardless of voltage.

Dependent Sources

  • Voltage-Controlled Voltage Source (VCVS): Voltage proportional to another voltage in the circuit.
  • Current-Controlled Voltage Source (CCVS): Voltage proportional to a current elsewhere.
  • Voltage-Controlled Current Source (VCCS): Current proportional to another voltage.
  • Current-Controlled Current Source (CCCS): Current proportional to another current.

Circuit Fundamentals

Understanding basic circuit laws is crucial for circuit analysis.

Ohm’s Law

  • Statement: The voltage across a resistor is directly proportional to the current through it.
  • Equation: V = IR

Kirchhoff’s Laws

Kirchhoff’s Current Law (KCL)

  • Statement: The sum of currents entering a node equals the sum of currents leaving the node.
  • Equation: ∑Iin = ∑Iout

Kirchhoff’s Voltage Law (KVL)

  • Statement: The sum of voltage drops around any closed loop is zero.
  • Equation: ∑V = 0

Techniques

Several techniques simplify circuit analysis.

Source Transformation

  • Voltage Source to Current Source: A voltage source (V) in series with resistor (R) can be transformed into a current source (I = V/R) in parallel with R.
  • Current Source to Voltage Source: A current source (I) in parallel with R can be transformed into a voltage source (V = IR) in series with R.

Passive Components in Series and Parallel

Resistors in Series

The equivalent resistance is the sum of individual resistances:

Req = R1 + R2 + R3 + ...

Resistors in Parallel

The reciprocal of the equivalent resistance is the sum of reciprocals:

1/Req = 1/R1 + 1/R2 + 1/R3 + ...
