Black Body Radiation & Planck’s Law

Black Body Radiation

A black body is an idealized object that absorbs all electromagnetic radiation that falls on it. When heated, it emits radiation across the entire spectrum.

Planck’s Law

This law describes the spectral density of electromagnetic radiation emitted by a black body in thermal equilibrium at a given temperature (T). Planck's formula is:

B(ν,T) = c² / (2hν³) * 1 / (e^(hν/kT) - 1)

Where:

  • B(ν,T) is the spectral radiance.
  • ν is the frequency of the radiation.
  • T is the absolute temperature of the black body.
  • h is Planck's constant (approx. 6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ Js).
  • c is the speed of light (approx. 3.00 × 10⁸ m/s).
  • k is Boltzmann's constant (approx. 1.38 × 10⁻²³ J/K).

Planck's law revolutionized physics by introducing the concept of quantized energy.

Photoelectric Effect & Einstein’s Equation

Photoelectric Effect

The emission of electrons from a metal surface when light shines on it.

Einstein’s Equation

Einstein explained the photoelectric effect by proposing that light consists of discrete packets of energy called photons. The energy of a photon is given by:

E = hν

Where:

  • E is the energy of the photon.
  • h is Planck's constant.
  • ν is the frequency of the light.

The kinetic energy (K) of the emitted electron is:

K = hν − ϕ

Where ϕ is the work function of the metal (the minimum energy required to remove an electron from the metal surface).

Compton Effect

The Compton effect refers to the scattering of a photon by a charged particle (usually an electron), resulting in a decrease in energy (increase in wavelength) of the photon. The change in wavelength (Δλ) is given by:

Δλ = λ' − λ = (h / (m_e * c)) * (1 − cos θ)

Where:

  • λ is the initial wavelength of the photon.
  • λ' is the final wavelength of the photon.
  • h is Planck's constant.
  • m_e is the mass of the electron (approx. 9.11 × 10⁻³¹ kg).
  • c is the speed of light.
  • θ is the scattering angle.

Wave-Particle Duality & de Broglie Hypothesis

Wave-Particle Duality

The concept that every elementary particle or quantum entity exhibits the properties of both particles and waves.

de Broglie Hypothesis

de Broglie proposed that particles also have a wavelength associated with them, given by:

λ = h / p = h / (mv)

Where:

  • λ is the de Broglie wavelength.
  • h is Planck's constant.
  • p is the momentum of the particle.
  • m is the mass of the particle.
  • v is the velocity of the particle.

Heisenberg’s Uncertainty Principle

Heisenberg’s Uncertainty Principle states that there is a fundamental limit to the precision with which certain pairs of physical properties of a particle, such as position and momentum, can be known simultaneously. Mathematically:

ΔxΔp ≥ ℏ / 2

Where:

  • Δx is the uncertainty in position.
  • Δp is the uncertainty in momentum.
  • ℏ is the reduced Planck's constant (ℏ = h / 2π).

Similarly, for energy and time:

ΔEΔt ≥ ℏ / 2

Schrödinger’s Wave Equation & Particle in Infinite Potential Box

Schrödinger’s Wave Equation

This is a fundamental equation in quantum mechanics that describes how the quantum state of a physical system changes over time. The time-independent Schrödinger equation is:

− (ℏ² / 2m) * (d²ψ(x) / dx²) + V(x)ψ(x) = Eψ(x)

Where:

  • ψ(x) is the wave function.
  • m is the mass of the particle.
  • V(x) is the potential energy function.
  • E is the energy of the particle.

Particle in Infinite Potential Box

A simple but important application of the Schrödinger equation. For a particle in a 1D box of length (L), the potential (V(x)) is 0 inside the box and infinite outside. The solutions to the Schrödinger equation are:

ψₙ(x) = (√2 / L) * sin( (nπx) / L )

And the energy levels are:

Eₙ = (n²π²ℏ²) / (2mL²)

Where n = 1, 2, 3, ... are the quantum numbers.
