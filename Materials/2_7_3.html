Correlation, Regression, and Time Series Analysis

1. Correlation

Karl Pearson's Correlation Coefficient

The Karl Pearson's correlation coefficient measures the strength and direction of the linear relationship between two continuous variables.

Formula:
r = (Σ(x_i - mean_x)(y_i - mean_y)) / sqrt(Σ(x_i - mean_x)^2 * Σ(y_i - mean_y)^2)

Properties:

  • -1 ≤ r ≤ 1
  • r > 0 indicates a positive correlation.
  • r < 0 indicates a negative correlation.
  • r = 0 indicates no linear correlation.

Spearman’s Rank Correlation Coefficient

Spearman's rank correlation coefficient measures the strength and direction of the association between two ranked variables.

Formula:
ρ = 1 - (6 Σd_i²) / (n(n²-1))
where d_i = difference between the ranks of each observation

Properties:

  • -1 ≤ ρ ≤ 1
  • Perfect positive correlation if ρ = 1.
  • Perfect negative correlation if ρ = -1.
  • No correlation if ρ = 0.

Problems:

  • Calculation of r and ρ given datasets.
  • Interpretation of correlation values.

2. Regression

Simple Linear Regression

Simple linear regression is used to model the relationship between a dependent variable (Y) and an independent variable (X) using a straight line.

Equation:
Y = a + bX
where:

  • a = intercept
  • b = slope or regression coefficient

Properties of Regression Coefficients:

  • The correlation coefficient r is the geometric mean of the two regression coefficients.
  • If one regression coefficient is greater than unity, the other must be less than unity.
  • The sign of regression coefficients is the same as the sign of r.

Multiple Linear Regression (Three Variables)

Models the relationship between one dependent variable and two independent variables.

Equation:
Y = a + b1X1 + b2X2
where:

  • a = intercept
  • b1 = regression coefficient for X1
  • b2 = regression coefficient for X2

Problems:

  • Finding regression lines and interpreting coefficients.
  • Prediction using the regression model.

3. Time Series Analysis

Components of Time Series:

  • Trend (T): Long-term movement in the data.
  • Seasonal Variation (S): Short-term, regular variation based on season or period.
  • Cyclic Variation (C): Long-term oscillations or cycles.
  • Irregular Variation (I): Random, unpredictable influences.

Models of Decomposition:

  • Additive Model: Y = T + S + C + I
  • Multiplicative Model: Y = T × S × C × I

Estimation of Trend:

Method of Moving Averages:

  • Smooths data by averaging adjacent observations over a specified period.
  • Removes short-term fluctuations to highlight long-term trends.

Steps:

  • Choose the period of the moving average (e.g., 3 years, 5 years).
  • Calculate the average of each set of observations.
  • Plot the averages to reveal the trend.

Fitting Mathematical Curves:

Straight Line (Linear Trend):

Y = a + bX

  • Use the least squares method to estimate 'a' and 'b'.

Second Degree Parabola (Quadratic Trend):

Y = a + bX + cX²

  • Use multiple regression techniques to estimate 'a', 'b', and 'c'.

Understanding correlation, regression, and time series analysis is critical for statistical modeling, forecasting, and decision-making in various fields such as economics, engineering, healthcare, and social sciences.
